Understanding the 15-day & 45-day Moving Average Chart

Purpose of This Graph

This chart helps you visualize smoothed price trends over time. Instead of looking at every daily price (which can be noisy and hard to interpret), moving averages (MAs) smooth out the data by averaging prices over a fixed window. This makes it easier to see the underlying direction of the market—whether a stock or asset is trending up, down, or sideways.

The chart plots two MAs simultaneously so you can compare short-term momentum (15-day) with longer-term trend (45-day). Where they cross provides potential entry or exit signals that traders and analysts have used for decades.

How to Read the Chart

  • Orange line (15-day MA): The average of the closing price over the last 15 trading days. It reacts faster to recent price changes because it uses a shorter window. When the price rises or falls, this line moves sooner.
  • Purple line (45-day MA): The average over the last 45 trading days. It represents the longer-term trend and is smoother. It filters out short-lived noise and shows a more stable view of where price has been heading.
  • Yellow dots (Crossover points): These mark exact moments when the 15-day and 45-day lines cross. A crossover happens when the short-term trend changes relative to the long-term trend—often interpreted as a shift in sentiment or momentum.

Y-axis: Price in dollars. X-axis: Time (date). The grid helps you align prices with specific dates.

How to Use It

Golden Cross ( bullish signal): When the orange line crosses above the purple line, some traders interpret this as a potential buy signal. It suggests short-term momentum has turned positive relative to the longer trend.

Death Cross (bearish signal): When the orange line crosses below the purple line, it can indicate a potential sell signal. Short-term momentum has weakened compared to the longer-term trend.

Caution: Crossovers are lagging indicators—they reflect what has already happened, not what will happen. They work best as one input among many, not as a standalone trading rule. Always consider context (e.g., news, fundamentals, market conditions).

Time Range: Shorter Is Often Better

For study and learning, shorter time ranges are more useful. With 1–3 years of data, you can see crossovers clearly, understand how they relate to price moves, and compare different market phases. Over decades, the chart compresses and crossovers become harder to distinguish; the lines can look flat and less informative.

Use the date filters above this chart to focus on a specific period (e.g., 1–2 years around a market event). This helps you connect what you see in the chart to real-world events and build intuition about how moving averages behave.

Glossary

  • Moving Average (MA): The average of closing prices over a fixed number of past days. Each day, the window slides forward and a new average is computed.
  • Crossover: The point where two moving average lines intersect.
  • Golden Cross: The short-term MA crosses above the long-term MA (often bullish).
  • Death Cross: The short-term MA crosses below the long-term MA (often bearish).
